Colin Martinez’s blood alcohol was four times the legal limit when he was pronounced dead in his home in January after being forced to drink vodka as part of a hazing ritual, according to a coroner's report released in March. 

The 18-year-old college freshman rushed Tau Delta this semester at Northern Arizona University, where senior frat leaders forced him and three other pledges to drink two bottles of vodka, enough alcohol to make each of them puke. 

Now, Martinez is dead and the frat leaders are facing criminal charges for the fatal hazing. This is just the latest in a culture of violence among college students.

Hazing is any humiliating, degrading, or dangerous activity required to join or maintain membership in a group, regardless of the person's willingness to participate. Often, senior members of an organization haze new members to assert hierarchy. 

Drug and alcohol toxicity are the leading causes of death for fatal hazing incidents in the United States, an analysis of deadly hazing incidents shows. Hazing has killed at least one person in America every year since 1959. 

Most often, college aged men are the victims. Since the turn of the century, 113 people have died in hazing-related incidents. An analysis of those incidents shows the leading cause of death to be toxicological, which is listed as the primary or secondary cause of death in 49.6% of the 113 deadly hazing incidents reported since 2000. Blunt force trauma follows closely behind, being named in 44.2% of cases.
